Fortiva Mastercard Application Funnel

Conversion-focused redesign that turned a brand-forward credit card page into a clearer, more action-oriented application experience.

+38% increase in Conversion Rate
-22% decrease in Bounce Rate
+18% increase in Apply-CTR
Objective

Rework the Fortiva Mastercard landing experience to communicate value faster, build trust earlier, and create a clearer path to application for users rebuilding credit.

Role

UI Designer
Conversion-focused design strategy

Team
Me PM DV
Timeframe

3 weeks

Overview

Fortiva Mastercard targets people rebuilding credit, with a growing focus on a younger audience that expects clarity and speed. The landing page needed to reduce uncertainty fast. The original experience was visually clean, but strategically weak, with a vague value proposition, flat hierarchy, and little guidance toward action. I redesigned the page to lead with clearer value, stronger hierarchy, earlier trust signals, and a more consistent call-to-action system across desktop and mobile.

Problem

The original page created brand polish, but it did not create enough decision-making momentum for a cautious credit-rebuild audience.

Who it's for

  • Consumers rebuilding credit
  • Users who are skeptical, fee-sensitive, and quick to leave if the offer feels unclear
  • Visitors evaluating the card quickly before deciding whether to apply

What was broken

  • Value proposition was unclear and not outcome-driven
  • No clear primary CTA
  • Messaging did not align with a credit-rebuilding audience
  • Trust signals were not surfaced early enough
  • Sections felt disconnected instead of building a guided path to action
  • Mid-page benefits were harder to scan than they needed to be
The headline was too broad and did not communicate a clear user benefit.

Solution

Each design change directly addressed a friction point in the original page. Instead of treating the hero like a brand statement, I rebuilt it as a high-clarity conversion entry point.

How it was improved

  • Headlines now carry more meaningful value-driven copy
  • I created a clear visual hierarchy so the primary action stands out immediately
  • Trust signals are introduced earlier and closer to conversion moments
  • Displaying clearer benefits makes the offer easier to understand at a glance
  • Mid-page content is organized into more scannable modules
  • Primary actions are reinforced across the page at key decision points
  • I added the Fortiva card into the hero image so the product was visible immediately
  • The angled graphic behind the woman and card creates a visual lead-in, guiding the eye toward the card and back toward the CTA area on desktop
The headline now leads with immediate value and sets expectations quickly.

What Changed

The redesign was not a visual refresh alone. It changed how the page introduced value, directed attention, and reduced hesitation for a cautious audience.

The hero shifted from broad brand language to a clearer, benefit-led message that explains the value of the card faster. The CTA system was simplified so one primary action stands out immediately, with supporting content arranged to guide the eye toward it. Trust cues were also moved earlier, and the card itself became part of the hero so users could connect the offer, the product, and the next step at a glance.

Results

The redesign created a clearer first impression and a more confident path to apply. By tightening the headline, establishing a stronger CTA hierarchy, and surfacing trust cues earlier, the page reduced friction at the exact points where hesitant users were previously dropping off.

Performance improved across the key conversion signals: conversion rate increased 38%, bounce rate dropped 22%, and apply click-through rate rose 18%. The strongest gains came from making the offer easier to understand immediately, rather than asking users to scroll before they understood why the card was relevant to them.

Learnings

This project reinforced that users rebuilding credit do not respond to broad brand language alone. They need the offer framed in practical, low-friction terms right away: what the card helps them do, why they should trust it, and what action to take next.

It also showed that conversion improvements did not come from adding more content, but from improving sequence and emphasis. Clear value framing, visible product cues, and trust placed near action points consistently outperformed the original layout.
