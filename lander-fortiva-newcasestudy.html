Fortiva Mastercard Application Funnel

I redesigned the Fortiva landing page so people could understand the offer faster, trust it sooner, and know exactly where to go next.

+38% increase in Conversion Rate
-22% decrease in Bounce Rate
+18% increase in Apply-CTR
Objective

Make the Fortiva Mastercard page easier to understand and easier to act on, especially for people who were rebuilding credit and comparing their options quickly.

Role

UI Designer
Landing page redesign

Team
Me PM DV
Timeframe

3 weeks

Overview

Fortiva Mastercard is for people rebuilding credit. That audience needs the page to be clear right away. They want to know what the card offers, whether it feels trustworthy, and what they should do next. The original page looked polished, but it asked users to do too much work. The main message was not clear enough, the calls to action competed with each other, and the page did not guide people through the decision. I redesigned it to make the offer easier to understand from the first screen and to keep the path to apply clear on both desktop and mobile.

Problem

The old page did not give users a strong reason to keep going. The headline was broad, the value proposition was weak, and the page did not make prequalification feel like the clear next step. For someone rebuilding credit, the experience needed to explain the benefit faster and build trust earlier.

Who it's for

  • People rebuilding credit
  • Visitors checking if they prequalify or responding to a mail offer
  • People who may have been overlooked by other card issuers
  • Users trying to decide quickly if the card fits their everyday spending needs

What was broken

  • The page did not make prequalification feel like a clear next step
  • The quick application and instant decision message was not easy enough to find
  • The copy did not connect strongly enough to people working toward credit goals
  • Trust cues and cardholder proof showed up too late
  • Everyday-use benefits were present, but not easy to scan
  • The page felt more like separate content blocks than one guided path to apply
The headline was too broad. It did not quickly tell someone why this card might be useful for them.

Page Flow

After reviewing the old page, I mapped the path from first impression to application. I wanted to see whether the page was answering the right questions in the right order: what is the offer, why should I trust it, and what should I do next?

The flow made the priorities clearer. The hero needed to explain the value faster, trust needed to show up earlier, and the main action needed to be easier to find throughout the page.

Redesign Approach

I treated the redesign less like a visual refresh and more like a cleanup of the decision path. The page needed to answer the user's questions in a better order.

How it was improved

  • I rewrote the top message so the value was easier to understand right away
  • I made one primary action stand out instead of asking users to choose between equal buttons
  • I moved trust cues closer to the moments where people were deciding whether to keep going
  • I made the benefits easier to scan at a glance
  • I broke the middle of the page into clearer sections
  • I repeated the apply action where it made sense, so users did not have to hunt for it
  • I brought the Fortiva card into the hero so the product was visible immediately
  • I used the hero art to pull attention back toward the card and the apply area
The new headline gets to the point faster and gives users a clearer reason to keep reading.

Attention Shift

The heatmap comparison helped validate the direction of the redesign. In the original hero, attention was split between a broad headline, generic lifestyle imagery, and two competing CTAs. In the redesign, attention moved toward the rewards message, the card, and the prequalification action.

Fortiva Mastercard Performance Improvements

The redesign changed the order of the story. Instead of leading with a broad brand message, the page now starts by answering what the card is, why it may be useful, and how to apply. By simplifying the CTA system, bringing the card into the hero, and moving trust cues higher on the page, the page performed better across the actions that mattered most: users stayed longer, clicked through more often, and moved toward prequalification with less hesitation.

Learnings

This project reminded me that users rebuilding credit are not looking for clever wording. They need clear, trustworthy messaging that explains what the card does, why it is relevant to them, and what happens if they decide to apply.

The biggest improvement came from reordering the page and making the next step easier to find, not from adding more copy. In future work, I’ll prioritize clarity, trust, and a single obvious action over extra content.
